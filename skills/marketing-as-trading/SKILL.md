# Marketing as Trading

How we think about marketing spend across all our projects. **Distilled from Fryd Wiatrowski's marketing-as-portfolio-management framework** — the clearest mental model for channel allocation we've found — and grounded with industry benchmarks.

The core premise: **a marketing leader is a portfolio manager.** Channels are strategies. Spend is capital. Your job is to allocate toward the highest risk-adjusted marginal return and pull from strategies that have decayed. Everything else follows from taking that seriously.

If you're reporting blended averages across channels and calling it strategy, you're not managing — you're narrating.

---

## 1. The one rule

**Optimize for the return on the next dollar, not the average return.**

Average ROI tells you how a channel has performed. Marginal ROI tells you what happens when you spend more. The two diverge the moment a channel starts to fill up — and they always fill up.

A channel returning 10x is almost by definition capacity-constrained. If it scaled, the return would already have been competed down to the market rate.

- A Facebook campaign scaling from $500/day to $4,000/day typically sees CPA rise 50-80%.
- Google Search at 80%+ impression share just bids up your own CPCs.
- The one Substack sponsorship converting at 8x tops out at one Substack sponsorship.

**The skill is not finding the one channel that wins. It's running a portfolio of small, decaying edges and rotating capital through them faster than they die.**

---

## 2. Every channel has a capacity ceiling

As you push more spend into a channel, cost per acquisition rises. You exhaust the cheap inventory, saturate the audience, and your next customer gets more expensive than your average one. This is not a bug. It's market impact — the more size you push, the worse your fills.

### How channels saturate

| Channel | What fills up | Warning sign |
|---|---|---|
| Paid search | Keyword volume in your category | Impression share >80%, CPC climbing with no new queries |
| Paid social | Audience pool at your targeting | Frequency >2-3x/week per user, creative fatigue |
| Influencer | Supply of relevant creators at your price | Moving to adjacent niches or down-tier creators to maintain volume |
| Content/SEO | Keyword space in your category | Keyword cannibalization, diminishing traffic per new article |
| Referral/WOM | Your existing happy user base | Participation rate dropping per cohort |
| Community | Size of the relevant community | Engagement per post declining, audience overlap with paid |

### Scaling without blowing up CPA

- Scale auction-based channels (Meta, Google) in **15-20% increments**. Jumping budget by more than 20% in one move reliably degrades CPA.
- When CPA rises past your ceiling (Year-1 LTV / target payback), stop scaling that channel and rotate to the next one.
- **70-20-10 allocation**: 70% to proven channels, 20% to channels with early signal, 10% to pure experiments. Kill experiments at 3x target CPA.

---

## 3. Payback period is the metric that matters

LTV:CAC tells you eventual return. Payback period tells you when you get the cash back. A 4:1 LTV:CAC with 30-month payback will kill a fast-growing company's cash position. Payback is the constraint you actually trade against.

### Benchmarks by segment

| Segment | Good payback | Great payback |
|---|---|---|
| B2C / consumer apps | <6 months | <4 months |
| SMB SaaS (<$15K ACV) | <12 months | <8 months |
| Mid-Market ($15-100K ACV) | <18 months | <12 months |
| Enterprise (>$100K ACV) | <24 months | <18 months |

- **Sub-6 months**: clear room to pour gas. Scale aggressively.
- **6-12 months**: healthy. Scale proven channels, test new ones.
- **12-18 months**: acceptable for higher ACV. Watch cash.
- **>18 months**: investor alarm territory. Fix unit economics before scaling.

### The blended payback trap

Blending organic traffic (near-zero CAC) with paid channels makes everything look efficient. **Always segment payback by channel and by cohort.** The blended number hides the channel that's bleeding and the channel that's printing.

## 4. Alpha vs beta — what your team actually produced

Not every channel starts from the same place. Pretending otherwise is how you misprice your own performance — and make bad hiring decisions.

**Beta** is growth you'd get without earning it:
- Category demand (everyone's searching for AI tools right now)
- Product virality (every Calendly invite is a free impression on a non-user)
- Inherited brand equity
- Network effects baked into the product

**Alpha** is what your team produces independent of those tailwinds:
- Channel discovery and optimization
- Creative that outperforms category benchmarks
- Faster experimentation and rotation
- Execution speed — killing decayed strategies before competitors notice

### How to tell the difference

The honest question: **"What growth disappears if we pause marketing for 3 months?"**

- A team that improves blended CAC 30% during a category boom mostly rode beta.
- A team that holds CAC flat in a saturated category mostly produced alpha.
- Same observable number, opposite signals.

### Apply this to your own projects

When evaluating a channel's performance, ask: how much of this result came from the product or the market, and how much came from what we did? If you can't separate them, you can't make good allocation decisions. Run holdout tests (pause a channel for 2-4 weeks in a specific geo) to see what persists without it.

## 5. Hiring: read resumes against the basis

Marketing resumes are mostly beta in disguise. The growth lead at a viral product posting great numbers may have been carried by the product's built-in distribution. The marketer at a product with no virality posting comparable numbers had to manufacture every dollar against the wind.

### The counterintuitive hire

You usually want the marketer who grew the harder-to-distribute product — even when the headline numbers look worse. They built the muscle. The viral-product marketer may have built the habit of taking credit for tailwinds.

### What to look for

- **Did they build systems or ride waves?** Ask what they built that outlasted them. Playbooks, creative engines, attribution infrastructure, experimentation frameworks.
- **Can they talk about marginal returns?** If they only speak in blended averages, they weren't managing a portfolio — they were reporting on one.
- **Do they know what they killed?** Good operators shut down channels and campaigns regularly. If everything they ever ran "worked," they weren't measuring properly.

## 6. The influencer split: ops + creative, not one person

The instinct is to hire one "influencer marketing person" to own the channel. That role is actually two distinct jobs, and combining them into one hire almost always means one half suffers.

### Ops track (Creator Operations)

Sourcing, vetting, outreach, contracts, payments, scheduling, compliance, content rights tracking. This is volume-driven and process-heavy. Scales with tooling and headcount.

**Hire for**: organization, deal management, relationship maintenance, spreadsheet rigor.

### Creative track (Creative Strategy)

Briefs, narrative angles, format selection, hooks, platform-native framing. The brief is the product spec — it determines whether the content converts or just gets views.

**Hire for**: taste, writing ability, understanding of what makes content work on each platform.

### The flywheel

Discover creators (ops) -> brief and produce content (creative) -> push winners into paid amplification (performance) -> measure revenue -> feed learnings back to creative. The tracks share data but own different stages.

## 7. Performance marketing is not beneath you

If your payback period is under 12 months and you are not running performance marketing at meaningful scale, you are leaving a known, positive-return, fast-recovering position on the table.

That's not brand taste. That's declining free money.

### When to run it

- Payback period is proven and healthy (see benchmarks in section 3)
- You have creative that converts (test with small budgets first)
- You can attribute accurately enough to trust the numbers

### When to hold off

- Unit economics are unproven or margins are too thin
- You don't have attribution in place — scaling blind burns cash
- The product still has retention problems (performance marketing amplifies leaky buckets)

## 8. Portfolio rotation — the actual job

The highest-returning strategies live in the long tail and they do not scale. The single perfect newsletter sponsorship. The one niche creator whose audience is exactly your ICP. The community thread that generates 200 signups overnight.

These are real. They are beautiful. And they top out at numbers that won't sustain growth past a certain stage.

**The portfolio manager's actual job:**

1. **Discover** new channels and micro-strategies constantly (10% experimental budget)
2. **Validate** with small spend — does it convert at acceptable CPA?
3. **Scale** winners in controlled increments (15-20% budget increases)
4. **Monitor** marginal returns as you scale — CPA creep is the signal
5. **Rotate** capital out before the channel fully decays, into the next validated strategy
6. **Kill** underperformers fast — sunk cost is not a reason to keep spending

### The rotation cadence

- Review channel-level marginal CPA **weekly** for paid channels
- Review organic/content/referral **monthly**
- Run a full portfolio review **quarterly** — which channels grew capacity, which decayed, what's next in the pipeline
- Always have 2-3 channels in "validation" stage so you have somewhere to rotate into

---

## 9. Applying this to small projects

You don't need a $1M budget for portfolio thinking. Even at $500/month:

- **Start with one paid channel and one organic channel.** Don't spread thin across five.
- **Measure per-channel payback from day one.** Even rough numbers beat blended averages.
- **The cheapest high-ROI strategies are the ones that don't scale** — and that's fine at your stage. A direct DM to 20 potential customers, a single podcast appearance, a well-placed community post. Run those first. They'll tell you what messaging converts before you put money behind it.
- **Graduate to paid only after you know what converts organically.** Paid amplifies your message — if the message doesn't work, paid just makes it fail faster and more expensively.

---

## 10. The checklist

Before recommending a channel strategy or evaluating performance, run through:

- [ ] Am I looking at marginal ROI or average ROI? (If average, redo the analysis)
- [ ] What's the payback period per channel, not blended?
- [ ] How much of this growth is beta (product/market) vs alpha (team-produced)?
- [ ] Is this channel near its capacity ceiling? What's the next 20% increase going to cost?
- [ ] Do I have channels in validation stage ready to rotate into?
- [ ] Am I declining performance marketing out of taste rather than math?
- [ ] For influencer: do I have both ops and creative covered, or am I asking one person to do both?
- [ ] Am I killing underperformers fast enough, or am I rationalizing sunk cost?
