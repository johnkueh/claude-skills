# Julian Storytelling Tips

How we tell stories — in writing and out loud — across talks, pitches, launches, demos, and long-form content. **Distilled from [Julian Shapiro's "How to tell great stories"](https://www.julian.com/blog/storytelling).**

Two ideas carry the whole skill, so learn them first:

> **Storytelling is the art of strategically withholding information.** Raise a question, then make them wait for the answer.
>
> **It's also the art of time dilation.** Stretch the moments that matter. Rush the ones that don't.

Everything below is a way to apply those two ideas, or a way to deliver them so people actually feel it.

Most of our surfaces are written, so each technique is tagged **[write]**, **[speak]**, or **[both]**. Withholding and time dilation are **[both]** — they're structure, not performance.

---

## 1. The four standards

Every story we tell should be:

- **Withholding** — It opens a loop it doesn't immediately close. The audience leans in *because* they don't have the answer yet.
- **Dilated** — The climax gets room to breathe. We slow down where it counts and skip what doesn't earn its place.
- **Felt, not reported** — We relive it, we don't recite it. If the teller doesn't feel it, the audience won't either.
- **Earned** — It ends somewhere — a lesson, a turn, a piece of wisdom. A story with no meaning is just an anecdote.

If a draft fails any of these, it's not a story yet. It's information.

---

## 2. The arc (five beats)

Almost every great story moves through the same five beats. Use them as a skeleton, not a script.

1. **The hero's perspective.** Anchor to one person — ideally you, or someone the audience can become. People transfer their identity onto a single protagonist, not a committee.
2. **Suspense + empathy.** Make us care about the hero, then put them in question. We don't worry about people we don't know.
3. **Goals and obstacles that produce change.** A want, a wall, and a cost. No obstacle, no story.
4. **A surprising, meaningful moment of triumph.** The turn should land harder than expected — that's what time dilation protects.
5. **A lesson that sticks.** End on the takeaway. This is the part people repeat to someone else.

Write the beats down before you write the prose. If you can't name beat 4, you don't have the story yet.

---

## 3. Hooks — raise a question, delay the answer **[both]**

A hook is a line that raises a question and refuses to answer it right away.

- The model line: **"It was the worst date of my entire life."** You instantly want to know *why* — and Julian's point is you *don't get told for a while*. That gap is the engine.
- The hook is a promise: *something is coming.* The longer you can hold the promise open without losing the audience, the more the payoff is worth.
- **[write]** First sentence does the work. Open on the question, the strange detail, or the moment of highest tension — never on setup, context, or "So I want to tell you about the time…"
- **[speak]** Hooks are premeditated, not improvised. Julian notes Neil deGrasse Tyson writes down nearly 100% of the stories he tells in interviews. Pre-write your opening line.

**Anti-pattern:** front-loading the conclusion. "I learned that persistence pays off, here's what happened" kills the loop before it opens. Lead with the question; save the lesson for the end (§2, beat 5).

---

## 4. Suspense = strategic withholding **[both]**

Suspense isn't a genre, it's a discipline of release. You decide what the audience knows and when.

- Reveal details in the order that maximizes curiosity, not the order they happened. Chronology is not the same as suspense.
- Every detail you hand over early is a detail that can't pull them forward later. Hold the "why" and the "what happened next."
- Plant the question early, answer it late, and let smaller questions open and close along the way.
- **Don't reveal everything upfront** is the single most common fix. If a paragraph explains before it intrigues, flip it.

---

## 5. Time dilation — stretch the climax **[both]**

Real time and story time are different clocks. Spend words and seconds in proportion to emotional weight, not real-world duration.

- **Slow down the turn.** The half-second before a jump scare is where the fear lives — not the scare itself. Find your story's "half-second before" and expand it.
- **Speed past the filler.** Days can pass in four words. Don't narrate logistics, travel, or setup at the same resolution as the climax.
- **[write]** Dilation is sentence length and detail density. Short, stacking sentences at the peak. Sensory detail only where it counts — describe the room when the room matters, not on the way in.
- **[speak]** Dilation is pacing and silence — see §7.
- Horror and blockbusters do this nakedly (slow-mo, drawn-out anticipation). You're doing the same thing with prose rhythm or vocal pacing.

---

## 6. Specificity beats abstraction **[both]**

Concrete, particular detail is what makes a moment felt instead of summarized.

- **"A pack of starving lions sprinted after him for half a mile."** "Half a mile" does the work — a vague "a long way" evaporates. Pick the number, the brand, the exact thing.
- One sharp sensory detail outperforms three adjectives. Choose the detail that *implies* the scene rather than listing the scene.
- Specificity is also where dilation lives: the details you choose to render are the moments you're choosing to stretch.
- Julian critiques his own early storytelling as **"clinical"** and **"dry."** Clinical = accurate but unfelt. The fix is almost always a concrete, human detail, not more precision.

---

## 7. Delivery: feel it first **[speak]**

This is the part that separates a good script from a story that lands. Delivery carries the emotional weight; structure only sets it up.

- **Blow your own mind.** Relive the emotion in real time as you tell it — be genuinely excited at the exciting part, shocked at the shock, awed at the wonder. "Listeners feed off this like sugar." A teller who's bored of their own story poisons it.
- **Mental smiling.** Imagine smiling ear-to-ear as you speak. It puts warmth, bounce, and spark into the words without you having to grin like a maniac.
- **Vocal rhythm.** Spoken storytelling is a form of music. Vary speed, volume, and enthusiasm. Use staccato lines for impact.
- **Silence is the cheap superpower.** "Silence is one of the easiest and most powerful ways to play up a moment." A held pause before the reveal does what no adjective can. Most people rush straight through the beat that needed three seconds of nothing.

---

## 8. Mirror neurons — make them feel it first **[speak]**

People physically echo the emotion they observe: they wince at a broken ankle, laugh when you laugh, yawn when you yawn.

- The audience **does not feel it in their bones unless it looks like you're feeling it first.** Your face and voice are the trigger.
- This is the neurological reason §7 works. You're not performing *at* them; you're giving their mirror neurons something to copy.
- **[write]** equivalent: the *hero* has to visibly feel it on the page. If the protagonist registers fear, relief, or awe, the reader catches it. A narrator who stays neutral leaves the reader neutral.

---

## 9. Charisma + dropping self-consciousness **[speak]**

- **The charisma formula: confidence + joy + love for the audience**, projected at the same time. When you embody all three, listeners lower their guard and their judgment.
- **Public solitude** (Stanislavski): behave as if you're alone even with everyone watching. The audience mirrors your comfort — they relax when you do, and tense up when you're visibly self-aware about your "eccentricities, insecurities, and weird hand movements."
- The missing ingredient is usually **joy.** Julian diagnoses his own flat tellings as "there was no joy in my voice." A true detail told joylessly still falls flat.

---

## 10. Prepare hard, sound unprepared **[both]**

> "Feel no shame in artificially preparing your stories, but be a good enough actor to make it seem like you didn't prepare at all."

- **Write the story down beforehand.** Preparation is non-negotiable, not cheating.
- **Memorize the beats, not the words.** Lock the five beats (§2) and your opening hook (§3). Rediscover the phrasing live — that's what produces the natural pauses, the small self-corrections, the feeling of someone *remembering* rather than *reciting*.
- **Never read it at them.** "Listeners recoil in boredom when someone reads a speech at them. But grab someone by the collar, look them in the eye, and tell them — and they'll be glued."
- **[write]** The same instinct applies: a piece that's been heavily structured should still read like a person talking, not a report being filed. Edit until the seams don't show.

---

## 11. Finding stories worth telling

You can't deliver your way out of a story with no meaning. Mine your own life for raw material:

- **Formative moments** — anything that changed you.
- **Painful moments** — these carry the most weight and the most empathy.
- **Triumphs and cringes** — the highs and the wince-worthy lows both work.
- **The memories you can't shake** — strong impressions are strong for a reason; the audience will feel it too.
- **Vulnerability raises intrigue.** Stories that reveal discomfort or weakness pull people in harder than stories where you look good throughout.

**Selection test:** does it end in inspiration, wisdom, or a real insight? If there's no takeaway, it's an anecdote — entertaining at best, forgettable at worst. Keep mining.

---

## 12. How Julian found all this (use the same trick)

Julian reverse-engineered the framework by **learning by inversion** — watching *bad* storytellers and cataloguing what made them dull, then flipping each one into a rule. When you're stuck on why a story isn't landing, do the same: find a flat example of the same thing and name exactly what it's missing. The fix is usually the inverse.

---

## 13. The 30-second checklist

Before you publish or rehearse a story, ask:

- [ ] Does the **first line raise a question** it doesn't answer?
- [ ] Is there **one hero** the audience can map onto?
- [ ] Is something **at stake** — a want, a wall, a cost?
- [ ] Have I **withheld the "why"** until late instead of explaining upfront?
- [ ] Have I picked the **one moment to stretch** — and given it room?
- [ ] Have I **cut the filler** so logistics don't read at climax resolution?
- [ ] Is there a **concrete, specific detail** doing the heavy lifting (the "half a mile")?
- [ ] Does it **end on a lesson** someone would repeat to a friend?
- [ ] **[speak]** Am I reliving the emotion, not reciting it? Is there joy in my voice?
- [ ] **[speak]** Did I plan where the **silence** goes?
- [ ] **[speak]** Do I know my **beats**, not my exact words?

## 14. Patterns we keep getting wrong

A running list. Add to it.

- **Opening with setup.** "So, a bit of background first…" — you've spent your hook on context. Open on the question.
- **Front-loading the lesson.** Telling them the takeaway before the story closes the loop nobody opened yet. Lesson goes last.
- **Chronological = suspenseful.** It isn't. Reorder for curiosity, not for the calendar.
- **Same resolution everywhere.** Narrating the drive to the airport with the same detail as the climax. Dilate the peak, skim the rest.
- **Clinical accuracy.** Correct, complete, and dead. Trade a precise abstraction for one concrete human detail.
- **Reciting, not reliving.** A true story told with no joy or emotion still flops. Delivery is not optional.
- **No silence.** Rushing straight through the reveal. The pause *is* the payoff.
- **Sounding prepared.** Reading it at people. Memorize beats, rediscover the words.
- **No meaning.** A vivid anecdote that ends nowhere. If you can't name the takeaway, keep it in the drawer.
